# Why I Chose Pentesting at 33 — My Honest Beginning into Cybersecurity

At 33, I didn’t expect to start over.

Life had other plans.

I went through a difficult personal phase. My mental health was breaking down, my family life was unstable, and I found myself questioning everything — my career, my direction, and what I truly wanted for the rest of my life.

I have an academic background in Economics — both BSc and MSc — and over the years, I’ve worked across different roles in Nigeria and in the UK: logistics, auditing, factory management, administration, customer service, and teaching. Out of all these, I enjoyed teaching and managerial roles the most. I liked solving problems, guiding people, and building systems that worked.

But something was still missing.

I wanted a skill that would give me freedom — the ability to work from anywhere, grow continuously, and build something meaningful. I’ve always had an interest in IT, and at one point, I even considered building a survey application. That curiosity slowly turned into something more serious.

When I returned home, I made a decision: I enrolled in a **Diploma in Cybersecurity**.

That decision changed everything.

## Discovering Pentesting

During my cybersecurity diploma, I was introduced to ethical hacking and penetration testing. At first, it felt intimidating. New terms, Linux commands, networking concepts — everything seemed unfamiliar.

But the more I learned, the more I became fascinated.

Pentesting felt like solving puzzles. It required patience, curiosity, and logical thinking — qualities I already had from my previous roles. I knew I had found something I genuinely enjoyed.

My goal became clear:  
I don’t just want to learn pentesting — I want to become very good at it. Good enough to teach others, and eventually become one of the best in my field.

## The Early Roadblocks

Like every beginner, I ran into several challenges:

- Setting up Kali Linux in VMware
    
- Network connectivity issues
    
- Understanding IP addressing
    
- Learning how ports and services work
    
- Interpreting Nmap scan results
    

At times, things didn’t work. My Kali machine had no internet. My target machine went offline. Commands returned unexpected results. But instead of getting discouraged, I started documenting everything.

This became one of the best decisions I made.

## Building My Learning System

I started using **Obsidian** to take structured notes. Every concept I learned, every command I tried, and every mistake I made — I wrote it down.

My notes covered:

- Network Basics
    
- Lab Setup (Kali + Metasploitable)
    
- Nmap First Scans
    
- Scan Types
    
- NSE Scripts
    
- Output Interpretation
    
- Troubleshooting Steps
    

Writing notes helped me slow down and truly understand what I was doing. It also gave me something valuable — my own personal knowledge base.

## Learning Nmap — My First Real Tool

Nmap became my first serious tool.

I started with simple scans:

- My first scan
    
- Scanning open ports
    
- Service detection (`-sV`)
    
- OS detection (`-O`)
    
- Ping sweeps
    
- Timing templates
    
- Script scanning
    

I learned how different scan types worked:

- TCP SYN scan
    
- UDP scan
    
- Xmas scan
    
- Ping sweep
    

I also explored **Nmap Scripting Engine (NSE)**, which opened another door. I began running scripts like:

- Default scripts (`-sC`)
    
- Vulnerability scripts (`--script=vuln`)
    
- SMB enumeration scripts
    

At first, I didn’t understand everything. But slowly, I began to recognize patterns in scan results. I started knowing what to look for — open ports, services, versions, and potential vulnerabilities.

That was a big milestone for me.

## TryHackMe — Structured Learning

To reinforce my learning, I started using **TryHackMe**, especially the **Nmap room**. It helped me understand:

- Scan types
    
- Timing templates
    
- NSE scripts
    
- Enumeration techniques
    

What I liked most was the hands-on approach. I wasn’t just reading — I was doing.

## Why I’m Doing This

My reasons for learning pentesting are simple and honest:

- I want a career I enjoy
    
- I want to work remotely long-term
    
- I want financial stability
    
- I want to travel again
    
- I want to rebuild my life
    

Most importantly, I want to prove to myself that starting over at 33 is not too late.

## Where I Am Now

So far, I’ve learned:

- Setting up my pentesting lab
    
- Network basics
    
- Nmap fundamentals
    
- Scan types
    
- NSE scripts
    
- Output analysis
    
- TryHackMe Nmap room
    

It’s still early, but I can already see progress.

My next steps:

- Exploitation basics

- Real-world labs
    

This is just the beginning.

I know the journey will take time, but I’m committed. My long-term goal is to become highly skilled, teach others, and eventually work remotely in cybersecurity.

Starting at 33 may not be common, but it’s my story — and I’m just getting started.
